How required income is calculated

For each standard, we take the binding constraint: required_income = max(PITI / front_end_dti, (PITI + other_debts) / back_end_dti). That's because lenders apply both ratios and you have to clear both. "Standard" 28/36 is the conventional underwriting baseline. FHA allows up to 31/43. Ramsey-style 25% is the most conservative — leaves room for everything else.

How the recommendation is calculated

Safe down payment = current liquid − closing costs − initial repairs − emergency fund (months × monthly expenses). If safe down payment ≥ planned down payment, you're in good shape. If safe down payment is lower, the recommended max down payment is what's left after preserving the emergency fund. We never recommend depleting below the emergency fund target.

How this analysis works
Housing as % of gross: Financial advisors recommend keeping housing below 28% of gross income (front-end DTI). Above 35% leaves little room for retirement savings.

Retirement contribution: Most advisors recommend saving 15-20% of gross income for retirement (including employer match). If housing consumes too much, you may fall short.

Opportunity cost: The difference between owning and renting, invested over time, shows what you give up in liquid retirement assets. However, home equity is also wealth — just illiquid.

Key tradeoff: A paid-off home reduces retirement income needs, but over-housing today can mean under-saving for retirement. Balance is key.

Math & caveats

Break-even (months) = closing costs ÷ monthly P&I savings. If you'll be in the home longer than break-even, refi pays off in cash terms. Lifetime interest saved compares total interest paid on the rest of the original loan vs. the new loan over its full term.

Notes: We compare P&I only (taxes, insurance, HOA, PMI are unchanged by refi). If you extend the term (e.g. refi a 25-yr-remaining loan into a fresh 30-yr), monthly payment drops but lifetime interest may rise. Cash-out increases the new loan size and shows up as "Cash received" — useful for home improvements but increases total interest. Rolling closing costs into the loan adds them to the new balance instead of paying upfront.

How these metrics work
1. Simple ROI = (Net Equity − Total Cash Invested) / Total Cash Invested
Shows total percentage gain/loss on all cash put into the home. Easy to understand but doesn't account for timing.

2. CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) = (Net Equity / Initial Cash)^(1/years) − 1
Annualized return on your initial outlay (down payment + closing). Useful for comparing to stock market returns, but ignores ongoing payments.

3. IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
The discount rate that makes the NPV of all cash flows equal zero. Accounts for the timing of every payment and the final sale proceeds. This is the most accurate measure of your true annualized return, and what real estate investors use.

Market comparison: Shows what you'd have if you invested the same cash flows (initial + monthly) in the stock market at your expected return rate.
